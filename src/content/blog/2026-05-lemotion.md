Have you ever noticed that those "try not to laugh" compilations are almost never *actually* funny? It's likely because the raw, spontaneous intention behind the content has been completely stripped away, leaving behind something that feels closer to brainrot than entertainment. This philosophy towards content can be applied to many different emotions like sadness, outrage, trauma, and even temptation. This was my team's idea for a startup competition that we called **"lemotion"**, an app that helps you reclaim your emotional agency.

Previously, I wrote about [free will](https://ai.meta.com/blog/tribe-v2-brain-predictive-foundation-model/), mentioning **TribeV2**, a predictive model for how our brains respond to video media. lemotion was partially inspired by that technology. Since we have models that can pretty much predict how someone might react to media, why not take advantage of those models in a protective way? Now, you can stop falling for ragebait. If you have trauma, you can prevent triggers. Overall, you can control your feed tailored to your own psychological response rather than a generalized social media algorithm. I really recommend watching our [pitch here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gU2Hbh1qn1U), or [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VwjZMvnJEoo) if you don't have much time.

We submitted our pitch and waited for the awards ceremony, but unfortunately, we didn't even become finalists. Some of the finalists for the competition included genAI video automation for social media and a GPT wrapper for learning magic tricks. This was my fourth competition, and since I won the last three in a row, I didn't think it was luck anymore, so it sucked when I saw that we didn't even make the finals. However, things don't end here. Despite losing, it was fun building the project with my teammates. The late-night grinding, the all-nighter before submission day just to make the pitch *that* much better, the almost daily meetings. If you have any feedback on our project, let me know. Watch our MVP demo!
