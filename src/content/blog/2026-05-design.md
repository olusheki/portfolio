I started working on this portfolio on February 11th, 2026, and it has grown to become one of my favorite things I’ve ever worked on. I love how it looks and feels to use, and I feel lots of ownership over it despite me generating the code with AI. But why do I feel such embodiment in AI-generated code? Simply because I had complete ownership over the vision.

Vision is a very important thing when you start making a project—what do you want it to look and feel like? What specific features you had in mind, what little things that make your project unique. When I first started, I already knew how I wanted my portfolio to look and feel like, and when I was done, I was able to take a layout that was in my mind and construct it in real life. 

Imagine my shock when I was casually scrolling on LinkedIn, and I saw someone post their portfolio, asking people to check it out. I decided to check it out, and then for a split second I thought I got redirected to my own portfolio! I’m not saying it was a copy, but it felt very eerily visually inspired—I didn’t even know people were seriously looking at my portfolio. And here’s an image just to show that I’m not exaggerating:

At first, I felt like something was taken from me or maybe I was just super generic, but then I quickly realized that that was the whole point of me making my portfolio anyway. I’ve always been taught to “steal like an artist”—nothing is truly original, not even my website. This just means that it made enough of an impression on someone that it inspired them to create something. 

Here’s how I stole like an artist to end up making my website, looking at my two major inspirations. Starting with [ertdfgcvb](https://ertdfgcvb.xyz/), aka [Andreas Gysin](https://www.instagram.com/andreasgysin/). He’s an artist who creates art, blending code and art to create digital and physical exhibits in procedural typography, particularly using ASCII art. I first discovered him and his work when I was doing my Technical Design internship at BrandNewSchool, and his work inspired my first project using animation in Houdini. He’s one of the more major inspirations for my website, particularly the more quirky and artistic elements. Please check out his work, and especially play around with the [ASCII playground](https://play.ertdfgcvb.xyz/) he made. 

My second inspiration, more towards the vibe of the website, is [Daniel Nguyen's Personal Website](https://www.dan1el.me/), via a LinkedIn post talking about it and inviting others to use theirs as a template. My friend had ended up using their template, so I checked out the original website, and I really liked how it wasn’t like a typical portfolio. Usually, every CS or Engineering student’s portfolio follows a flow where it’s an image of their face name, and all quick info (eg. CS @ this school, accolades, desired role, etc). Then you scroll a bit with some laggy animations and neon glowing components and see experience, scroll, project 1 → … → project n, and just keep on scrolling endlessly asking yourself, “when will this end?” I like how it wasn’t “trying too hard” and also the fact you don’t need to scroll at all. I took the LeetCode card from there too, and I liked the open source mentality, so I did the same with my portfolio. It’s currently in a public repo with a template guide in the README.

So basically, what this experience taught me about making impactful design is to create something new with the things that impacted **you** the most. Save your references so you’re not trying to find them again later. Lastly, Don’t throw art to the side. It’s everywhere, and it’s super important (especially in tech).
