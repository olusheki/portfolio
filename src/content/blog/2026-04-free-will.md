I used to believe in free will. I thought, "Why wouldn't I have free will? Nothing can stop me from doing anything." I only saw it as the ability to act willingly, even when it goes against social norms. The idea that "I can do anything I want", like screaming in a library or challenging social taboos, seemed like undeniable proof of this. However, after exploring the link between technology and biology, I have started to rethink this idea. It has forced me to question my confidence and broaden my view of this philosophical debate.

I first started thinking when I came across a digital simulation of a fly, based on its real biology. Eon Systems PBC, which focuses on digital brain emulation, has provided surprising evidence of concept with its digital model of a fly. By mapping the fly's brain, Eon created a predictive model to interpret over 140,000 neurons and connections firing into complex behaviors like grooming, foraging, and movement. Although this technology isn't perfect yet, it shows a clear way toward fully simulating a complex biological being.

I started fully challenging my initial take when I came across this: there's Meta's research on a Brain Predictive Foundation Model called Tribe V2. This model forecasts how humans respond to things around them, especially media. It's advanced enough to visualize which areas of the brain are activated. It's easy to forget that flies and mice are usually the pilots of this type of technology, but there's nothing stopping us from applying it to humanity. This raises ethical concerns about the potential to craft media designed to trigger specific responses or manipulate people's behavior on a large scale. Following this logic, it's easy to imagine a future where a complete human (every neuron, nerve, and muscle) could be mapped and placed into a flawless simulation.

This is where the free will argument comes in. If we can completely simulate human thoughts, reactions, and instincts, why couldn't we predict an entire life? If our "actions" are just reactions and instinct (two things we don't control) following a complex yet deterministic biological and environmental script, the idea of free will falls apart. We are products of our environment, and moving from one setting to another is just a predicted change in input, not a real choice. If it's possible to perfectly replicate a life in a simulation, it suggests that my future is already a certain outcome based on my initial conditions. To predict a life, one must view it through the lens of linear algebra. We turn reality into a matrix, where every human outcome is a vector determined by countless weighted variables, reducing the totality of existence to a solvable equation.

The thought of an all-encompassing human simulation is quite dystopian, but I will admit the convenience is appealing. Imagine an AI acting as a "second brain," handling tasks like drafting emails, writing applications, or dealing with boring personal work. This model would understand my thoughts and desires, reducing my need to provide context for every AI output. But does this convenience justify giving up the chance for real agency? I find myself valuing the personal, non-technical parts of my life that rely on my unique character. True human interactions, small gestures that reveal character, and the unpredictable nature of authentic connection—these are where free will, if it exists, is most clearly seen. The future may look predictable for an algorithm, but for now, I choose to embrace the unpredictable beauty of human character.
